# RETROSPECTIVE
## Hiring Ahead of Revenue — The 2023 Headcount Mistake

**Author:** Priya Patel, CTO  
**Date:** 2023-10-01  
**Type:** Strategic retrospective — not a technical incident  
**Outcome:** Hired 4 engineers and 1 designer in H1 2023 before revenue supported it. Laid off 2 engineers in September 2023. Runway extended by 5 months. Team trust significantly damaged.  

---

### What Happened

In January 2023, following promising growth metrics in Q4 2022, the founding team made the decision to aggressively hire engineering talent in anticipation of a Series A closing in Q2 2023. Between February and May 2023, we hired:

- 2 senior backend engineers
- 1 senior frontend engineer  
- 1 data engineer
- 1 product designer

Total additional monthly burn: $62,000.

The Series A did not close in Q2. Investor feedback was that ARR growth had slowed and we hadn't demonstrated clear enterprise motion. By July 2023, we had 4.2 months of runway at the new burn rate. We needed to act.

In September 2023, we let go of 2 of the engineers hired in this cohort. The layoffs affected people who had joined in good faith 4–6 months earlier, uprooted their lives, and turned down other offers.

The remaining team was shaken. Two engineers from the original founding team had visible drops in productivity and engagement for 6–8 weeks post-layoff. One explicitly said in their 1:1 that they were "updating their LinkedIn, just in case."

---

### Why We Hired

The reasoning at the time:

1. "We need the capacity to build what the enterprise customers want."
2. "Good engineers are hard to find — we should hire them when we can."
3. "The Series A will close and we'll need to move fast."

All three of these were true. None of them justified hiring ahead of the revenue that would support those salaries.

---

### What We Got Wrong

**We hired for the company we hoped to be, not the company we were.**

At the time of the hiring decision, our ARR was $420K. We were adding $18–22K ARR per month. The new hires added $62K/month to burn. The math only worked if growth accelerated significantly and the Series A closed.

Neither condition was guaranteed. We treated both as near-certain.

**We confused "we have the money now" with "we can afford this."**

At the time of hiring, we had 14 months of runway. It felt like security. But hiring changes your burn rate permanently until you either grow into it or cut. 14 months at old burn rate became 8 months at new burn rate became 4.2 months too fast.

---

### The Human Cost

Two people lost their jobs because of a financial decision made before they were hired. They had no input into that decision. This is the part that doesn't show up in runway calculations.

When we do layoffs, we're not just adjusting a spreadsheet. We're ending someone's employment, affecting their mortgage, their visa status in some cases, their sense of professional identity. We made that happen to people we recruited.

The reputational cost is also real: word gets around. Our Glassdoor rating dropped. Two candidates we were in process with withdrew.

---

### What Changes

**Rule: Headcount decisions require a revenue trigger, not a hope.**

Going forward, any new engineering hire must be justified by one of:

1. **Signed ARR to support it** — the revenue is in the bank, not projected
2. **Closed funding round** — money received, not term sheet signed
3. **Priya + board approval with explicit runway impact documented**

"We expect to close the Series A" is not a trigger. "The Series A closed and funds are received" is a trigger.

See `Hiring_Policy.md` for the full framework.

---

### The Lesson

> "We hired 5 people in 4 months because we were optimistic. We let 2 of them go 6 months later because we were over-extended. The optimism cost two people their jobs and cost the rest of the team their sense of security. Hire when the revenue is real, not when you believe it will be."  
> — Priya Patel
